Design Principles

Below are principles that have guided the CodeX HL7 FHIR Accelerator Community and in the design, planning, development, testing, adoption, and value of FHIR-based data standards, including mCODE. These principles guide the recommendations found in the Playbook Tracks and Case Studies on this website. If additional specialties follow the principles, the resulting standards are more likely to be developed efficiently, to be compatible across specialties as part of each patient's life-time health, to be adopted in practice, and to provide value for patients and all stakeholders.

  1. A dedicated and diverse Community is essential to the design and execution of a health data standards project
    • The Community must include all or a subset of patients, providers, health systems, Gov’t, vendors, payers, and others, depending on the Use Case.
    • Community members will plan Use Cases, design and develop solutions, and drive impactful adoption to that solve specific Community problems.
  2. Compelling Use Cases are the foundation upon which solutions are designed and driven.
    • Define each Use Case as a well-defined project to address one important and narrowly-focused problem.
    • Develop a plan that is (ultimately) end-to-end - including the scope of work, resources required, a minimal set of data concepts to be standardized, and a pragmatic strategy for how standards will be implemented, tested, adopted, and provide value in the real world.
    • Prioritize Use Cases that address serious problems. However, start with Use Cases that have commitment from all or almost all of the Community support and resources needed to succeed.
    • Prioritize Use Cases that improve collection and sharing of quality, core, standardized data related to patient care and outcomes should be the top priority. Patient care data will make major contributions to secondary uses, such as research, quality measurement, payer operations, regulatory requirements, personal use, etc.
  3. Clinical information requirements are a first priority for each Use Case.
    • Build consensus through a diverse team of clinial experts on the minimal data requirements needed to address core Use Case requirements and which could be useful for other Use Cases, as well.
    • Define clinical requirements as discrete observations and actions (e.g., patient birth date = 11/04/1989 vs. patient is 34 years old).
    • If previous work in the space exists, seek to work with the other initiatives and if it makes sense, adopt/adapt their work rather than re-do it.
    • Get input and review through clinical specialists and patients first, then other medical, standards, vendor, and/or regulatory experts, as appropriate for the Use Case.
  4. FHIR Implementation Guides (IG) instruct developers on how to send and receive the clinical data requirements
    • A specialty should aim for a single, core IG that represents the minimial clinical requirements for set of Use Cases common within the specialty. The core IG will be appended and improved as Use Cases are executed. Supplemental IGs can be built when new data requirements are not considered core, but important for smaller Communities and/or edge applications.
    • Leverage Government-backed interoperability standards whenever possible, e.g., United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI), Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR), US Core FHIR Implementation Guide, and others. Gov’t support increases motivation for adoption and thus provides a stronger basis for cross-specialty interoperability.
    • Leverage the available tools for modeling and IG development, to produce higher-quality FHIR IGs that are more coherent across specialties, Use Cases, and projects.
    • Leverage global code systems and terminology standards (e.g., LOINC, SNOMED CT, CPT, etc.).
    • Not all data elements represented in an IG will be collected for every patient during every encounter, since people and their disease states differ. When data should be collected, the standard provides the template.
    • Standardize work through an open, royalty-free health standards development organization, preferrably Health Level Seven (HL7).
  5. Work within a vision of a coherent lifetime Standard Health Record for every patient, even while focusing on each narrowly-scoped Use Case.
    • Work to enable standardized data to become the basis for every patient's health record and standard of care.
    • Collect patient data once (generally around the encounter). Data collected can then be reused for multiple Use Cases going forward, wiht minimal or no re-mapping.
    • Data originally collected for one patient's health event can be important for future events, including different health disease states.
    • Work to enable consistent representation of data elements that could be used across specialties, subspecialties, and Use Cases. Different specialties, subspecialties, and Use Cases within a specialty often require the same data concept that could be modelled differently (and perhaps have been described differently, historically).
    • Leverage principles, templates, and cross-specialty processes that reconcile differences and consolidate solutions in one place, where possible.
    • Patients are increasingly aware of and their health data and options for its collection, storage, and use. Work to empower Patients to be engaged with and have control of their health data.
